ROE & ROCE Relationship (Part 1)

Understanding the structural relationship between Return on Equity (ROE) and Return on Capital Employed (ROCE) is vital for evaluating core business profitability vs financial leverage.

Core Definitions

$$\text{ROE} = \frac{\text{Net Income}}{\text{Shareholders' Equity}}$$

$$\text{ROCE} = \frac{\text{EBIT}}{\text{Capital Employed}} = \frac{\text{EBIT}}{\text{Equity} + \text{Total Debt} - \text{Non-Operating Assets}}$$

1. The Financial Leverage Bridge

ROCE measures how efficiently a business generates operating profit ($\text{EBIT}$) from all capital invested (debt + equity). ROE measures what is left over for equity holders after paying interest and taxes.

The relationship can be defined mathematically as:

$$\text{ROE} \approx \left[ \text{ROCE} + \left( \text{ROCE} - \text{Cost of Debt} \right) \times \frac{\text{Debt}}{\text{Equity}} \right] \times (1 - \text{Tax Rate})$$

  • Positive Financial Leverage ($\text{ROCE} > \text{Cost of Debt}$): Adding debt increases ROE above ROCE. The company borrows cheaply and invests at higher returns.
  • Negative Financial Leverage ($\text{ROCE} < \text{Cost of Debt}$): High debt burdens drag ROE far below ROCE, creating financial distress.

ROE & ROCE Relationship (Part 2)

2. The 4-Quadrant Diagnostic Matrix

Combination Diagnosis & Institutional Meaning
High ROCE / High ROE Elite Compounder: High business quality, moat, clean balance sheet. Generates organic growth without excessive debt.
Low ROCE / High ROE Financial Engineering Red Flag: Weak underlying operations masked by heavy debt or optical equity reductions (buybacks/losses). High risk!
High ROCE / Low ROE Cash Hoard / Tax Drag: High operating performance suppressed by massive idle cash on the balance sheet or expensive tax regimes.
Low ROCE / Low ROE Capital Destroyer: Poor pricing power, weak asset utilization, and value destruction across all capital suppliers.

3. Working Capital & Trade Payables Impact

In asset-light platforms or retail models (e.g., FMCG, market infra), negative working capital (high trade payables) reduces $\text{Capital Employed}$, resulting in astronomical ROCE figures. In zero-debt firms, ROE and ROCE track almost identically post-tax.

Balance Sheet Analysis

Q: If company receivables increase, is it a red flag?

An increase in accounts receivable (AR) can be a red flag, but it depends on the context[cite: 2]:

  • Potential Red Flags: Strained cash flow (struggling to collect payments), aggressive revenue recognition to inflate profits, or deteriorating credit quality by extending credit to less creditworthy customers[cite: 2].
  • Not Necessarily a Red Flag: Normal business growth (especially B2B), seasonal sales patterns, or strategic improvement in payment terms to attract customers[cite: 2].

Key Metrics to Analyze

  • Days Sales Outstanding (DSO): Increasing DSO indicates slower collections[cite: 2].
  • AR Turnover Ratio: Declining ratio means less efficiency in collections[cite: 2].
  • Bad Debt Expense: Rising expenses indicate collectability issues[cite: 2].

Cash from Operating Activity

CFO shows how much cash a company generates from its core business operations[cite: 2].

Formula

$$\text{CFO} = \text{Net Income} + \text{Non-Cash Exp.} \pm \Delta \text{Working Capital}$$

Red Flags in CFO

  • Negative Cash Flow: Poor profitability or inefficient operations[cite: 2].
  • Discrepancy with Net Income: Aggressive revenue recognition (booking sales before cash is collected)[cite: 2].
  • Increasing AR: Customers delaying payments[cite: 2].
  • Rising Inventory: Overproduction or difficulty selling products[cite: 2].
  • Declining Accounts Payable: Paying suppliers too quickly, straining cash flow[cite: 2].
  • Volatile CFO: Unstable revenue streams or poor working capital management[cite: 2].

Stock Market Fundamentals

Understanding the Indian market differs from the US market[cite: 3].

  • Company Lifecycle: Nascent, growth, sustainability/maturity, decline[cite: 3]. In the growth phase, positive cash flow is less critical, but later, margins, ROE, ROCE, and cash conversion matter[cite: 3].
  • Market Cycles: In bear markets, look for sustainability; in bull markets, people buy growth at any price[cite: 3].
  • Philosophy: It is all about managing risk-to-reward[cite: 3]. Charlie Munger likens poor risk management to "picking up pennies in front of a road roller"[cite: 3].

DuPont Analysis (Growth vs ROE)

A company growing top-line at 40% with a 10% ROE can see its ROE structurally increase as operating leverage kicks in[cite: 3].

Sustainable Growth Rate

$$\text{SGR} = \text{ROE} \times (1 - \text{Payout Ratio})$$

DuPont Equation

$$\text{ROE} = \text{Net Profit Margin} \times \text{Asset Turnover} \times \text{Equity Multiplier}$$

  • Operating Leverage: As revenue scales over a fixed cost base, net profit margins expand, pulling ROE upward[cite: 3].
  • Asset Efficiency: As capacity utilization improves, Asset Turnover spikes[cite: 3].
  • Capital Structure: Funding growth through debt increases the Equity Multiplier, magnifying equity returns but adding risk[cite: 3].

Mental Models & Philosophy (I)

Investing is ultimately about managing risk (risk to reward ratio)[cite: 3]. Core frameworks help improve rational decision-making.

  • Opportunity Cost: By choosing one investment or action, you forfeit the benefits of the alternatives. Every choice has a hidden cost, and capital deployment must always be measured against the next best alternative.
  • Business Longevity: Apply Porter's Five Forces and evaluate business models (philosophies of Warren-Munger, Peter Lynch, Aswath Damodaran, Fisher, Peter Thiel) to understand which businesses sustain and which collapse[cite: 3].
  • Circle of Competence: Identify the boundaries of your understanding. The size of your circle isn't as important as knowing exactly where the perimeter is.
  • Inversion: "Tell me where I'm going to die, so I'll never go there." Approach problems backwards to avoid failure rather than just seeking brilliance.
  • Margin of Safety: Always build a buffer. If you think a bridge needs to hold 10 tons, build it to hold 30 tons.

Mental Models & Philosophy (II)

Process vs. Outcome

Michael Mauboussin highlights that in probabilistic fields like investing, you must evaluate the process, not just the isolated outcome.

Good Outcome Bad Outcome
Good Process Earned Success
(Keep doing it)		 Bad Luck
(Stick to the process)
Bad Process Dumb Luck
(Dangerous)		 Poetic Justice
(Change process)
  • Second-Order Thinking: First-order thinking is simplistic. Second-order asks, "It's a good company, but everyone knows it, therefore the stock is overpriced."
  • Pareto Principle (80/20 Rule): Roughly 80% of consequences come from 20% of causes. Focus on the vital few business metrics that drive the compounding.
